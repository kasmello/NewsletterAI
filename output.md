# 🇮🇩 THE INDONESIA CORRUPTION WATCH NEWSLETTER
### Your Essential Briefing on Indonesia's Anti-Corruption Crisis

---

**SPECIAL REPORT | 2025-2026 EDITION**

---

Eighty years after independence, Indonesia finds itself at a crossroads. A nation of 280 million people — the world's fourth largest democracy — is grappling with a corruption crisis so severe it has triggered street protests, prison sentences, and one of the steepest falls in global integrity rankings in recent memory. From billion-dollar fuel fraud to teargas-filled streets, here are the five developments you need to know right now.

---

## 1. 🛢️ PERTAMINAGATE: THE $17 BILLION FUEL HEIST

Imagine paying premium prices for premium fuel — and quietly receiving something far inferior. That is exactly what Indonesians experienced for five years.

In February 2025, prosecutors unveiled a jaw-dropping scheme inside **Pertamina**, Indonesia's state-owned energy giant. Between 2018 and 2023, six Pertamina executives allegedly conspired to import crude oil at artificially inflated prices, adulterate premium-grade Pertamax fuel with cheaper Pertalite, and bill consumers for quality they never received. Estimated state losses? A staggering **IDR 193.7 trillion — up to $17 billion USD**.

By February 2026, nine defendants — including former subsidiary CEOs — received sentences of **9 to 15 years in prison**. PertaminaGate now ranks among the largest corruption cases in Indonesian history, exposing catastrophic governance failures at the heart of the nation's state-owned enterprise sector.

---

## 2. 📉 INDONESIA'S WORST CORRUPTION RANKING IN YEARS

Numbers rarely lie. In February 2026, Transparency International delivered a damning verdict: Indonesia **plummeted 10 places** in the 2025 Corruption Perceptions Index, landing at **109th out of 182 countries** with a score of just 34 out of 100 — a sharp collapse from its 2024 score of 43.

Indonesia Corruption Watch (ICW) pointed directly at **President Prabowo Subianto's administration**, citing weakened oversight mechanisms and politically motivated interference with anti-corruption institutions. The US State Department echoed those concerns, noting that restrictions on the Corruption Eradication Commission (KPK) had triggered a measurable decline in enforcement activity — enough to give foreign investors serious pause.

The message from the global community was unmistakable: Indonesia is moving in the wrong direction.

---

## 3. 🔥 BLOOD ON THE STREETS: PROTESTS SHAKE THE NATION

Indonesia's crisis was never just a bureaucratic story. It became deeply human in August 2025.

Sparked by the **"Gelap Indonesia" (Dark Indonesia) movement**, nationwide protests erupted as students, workers, and activists stormed Jakarta's parliament building. Their grievances were visceral — lawmakers proposing **$3,000 monthly allowances** for themselves while ordinary Indonesians endured austerity cuts and inflation. When **21-year-old delivery driver Affan Kurniawan** was killed in a police crackdown, outrage boiled over.

By September 4, 2025, Indonesia's National Human Rights Commission confirmed **10 deaths** linked to the unrest. Analysts called it a democratic referendum — citizens holding a mirror to a government they believed had betrayed them. It was the most significant political upheaval Indonesia had seen in years.

---

## 4. 🏛️ PRABOWO'S ANTI-CORRUPTION PLEDGE — AND THE SCEPTICS

On Indonesia's **80th Independence Day**, President Prabowo Subianto stood before the nation and declared war on corruption. He promised to fight "greednomics," cited **IDR 13.25 trillion recovered** from a palm oil corruption case, and warned officials against illegal activities including mining rackets.

The words were powerful. The reception was cold.

Civil society groups, opposition figures, and ICW immediately called out the glaring contradiction: how does a government fight corruption while simultaneously **dismantling the institutions** built to prosecute it? With the KPK's independence stripped by 2019 legislative amendments, critics argued Prabowo was fighting graft with one hand while tying the other behind the watchdog's back. Rather than calming protests, his speech appeared to accelerate them.

---

## 5. 💸 KPK SOUNDS THE ALARM: IDR 25 TRILLION AND CLIMBING

In July 2025, the KPK delivered sobering testimony to Indonesia's Constitutional Court: corruption losses handled by the commission between 2018 and 2025 had exceeded **IDR 25.1 trillion** — and the figure grows every single year.

Despite securing over **360 convictions** and recovering $80 million in assets, the KPK remains a diminished institution, hamstrung by vague legislation and executive oversight that critics say creates prosecutorial blind spots. A new UNODC partnership launched in early 2025 signals international alarm about Indonesia's capacity to handle sophisticated financial crimes.

As The Jakarta Post observed as Indonesia turned 80: the anti-corruption fight remains "**unfinished business**."

---

## CONCLUSION: INDONESIA AT THE CROSSROADS

The pattern across all five stories is impossible to ignore. Indonesia is not simply struggling with isolated corruption cases — it is experiencing a **systemic erosion of accountability**, where the tools designed to fight graft are being quietly hollowed out while grand anti-corruption rhetoric fills the airwaves.

The question facing Indonesia is no longer whether corruption exists. It is whether the political will — and the institutional muscle — exists to genuinely confront it. The streets, the rankings, and the courts are all delivering the same verdict.

**The Indonesian people are watching. And increasingly, so is the world.**
